# Game Ideation Strategies

Techniques for pushing game ideas beyond the obvious during the Expansion phase.

## The "Five Whys" of Possibility

For every game mechanic or concept, ask "what comes after this?" five times:

1. Cave exploration game — deeper caves with harder enemies
2. — underground civilizations with their own cultures and trade
3. — through the planet's core into a mirror world on the other side
4. — the planet is exhausted, launch into space to find new worlds
5. — each new world has its own underground to explore with alien geology

The first answer is what every game designer thinks of.
The fifth answer is where the magic lives.

## Dimension Discovery

Every game idea has dimensions most designers never consider. Probe each:

- **Core loop feel**: What does the player DO every 30 seconds? Is that action inherently satisfying?
- **Emotional arc**: How does the player FEEL across a session? Tension → mastery → surprise → triumph?
- **Social dynamics**: How do players interact, compete, cooperate, show off, grief, help?
- **Meta-game**: What keeps players coming back after "finishing"? Daily runs, leaderboards, community challenges, legacy systems?
- **Economy**: What resources flow? What creates meaningful scarcity and interesting trade-offs?
- **Emergent narrative**: What stories emerge naturally from the mechanics? "You won't believe what happened in my run..."
- **Surprise factor**: What would make a player stop and say "wait, you can DO that?"
- **Failure states**: Can dying/losing be interesting, not just punishing? Does failure teach something or change the world?
- **Progression feel**: Does power growth feel earned? Are there meaningful plateaus and breakthrough moments?
- **Player identity**: Can players express themselves? Build order, playstyle, cosmetics, naming, creativity?
- **Time scale**: What happens over minutes vs. hours vs. days vs. weeks? How does the game evolve?
- **Discovery**: What secrets, hidden mechanics, or "iceberg" depth exists for dedicated players to find?

## Cross-Pollination

Steal mechanics from unrelated game genres:

- A farming game could borrow diplomacy mechanics from grand strategy.
- A city builder could borrow narrative branching from RPGs.
- A puzzle game could borrow economic systems from trading sims.
- A combat system could borrow rhythm mechanics from music games.
- A survival game could borrow base defense from tower defense.
- An exploration game could borrow cooking/crafting combination systems from Breath of the Wild.

Ask: "What genre has solved a similar design problem in a completely different way?"

## The Uniqueness Test

For every major game mechanic, ask:

- Has this been done in another game? Which one?
- If yes, what makes our version genuinely different — not just "better balanced" or "prettier"?
- If the answer is "we do it the same but in our game" — that's not unique. Push harder.
- Could this mechanic be the reason someone plays THIS instead of the 50 similar games on Steam?

## The Fun Test

Before a mechanic survives Expansion, it must pass:

- **Verb test**: Can you describe what the player DOES in one active verb? ("dig", "combine", "dodge", "negotiate")
- **Choice test**: Does this create interesting decisions, or is there always an obvious best option?
- **Story test**: Will players tell each other about moments created by this mechanic?
- **Repeat test**: Is this fun the 100th time, not just the 1st? What changes to keep it fresh?

## Scope Explosion (Intentional)

During expansion, deliberately imagine the BIGGEST possible version:

- What if there were 100 biomes instead of 10?
- What if the world was persistent and other players could discover your creations?
- What if the game continued after the "ending" with an entirely new phase?
- What if the core mechanic applied to everything — combat, building, dialogue, crafting?
- What if failure was just as interesting as success — a whole parallel progression?

Not all of this will survive Critique — and that's the point.
Generate ambitious raw material, then refine down to the best ideas.

## Anti-Patterns in Game Ideation

| Avoid | Do Instead |
|-------|------------|
| "We could add multiplayer" (generic) | "Players discover each other's abandoned mines and can scavenge resources, leaving messages carved into cave walls" (specific, evocative) |
| Adding mechanics because competitors have them | Adding mechanics because they create unique player choices |
| Listing features without explaining WHY they're fun | Every mechanic needs a "this creates interesting play because..." |
| Stopping when you have "enough" features | Stopping when you have mechanics that make you genuinely excited to play |
| Playing it safe with proven formulas | If no mechanic makes you nervous about scope, you haven't pushed far enough |
| Designing systems in isolation | Every system should create interesting interactions with at least one other system |
